# Pirkei Avot 5 (Ethics of the Fathers), selections

*Pirkei Avot* ("Chapters of the Fathers") is a tractate of the **Mishnah**, the
earliest written layer of rabbinic teaching, compiled around **200 CE** by Rabbi
Judah ha-Nasi. It collects the maxims of the sages from roughly the third century
BCE through the second century CE. Chapter 5 is the famous "numbers" chapter:
almost every saying in it is a *counted list* — ten of this, ten of that, then
sevens, then fours. These are the people closest to the Hebrew text, and this is
how they handled it: by counting its patterns.

The mishnayot below are the ones we read in **Session 2**. English is Dr. Joshua
Kulp's translation (CC BY); see provenance at the foot of the page.

---

## 5:1 — The world was made by ten utterances

> **בַּעֲשָׂרָה מַאֲמָרוֹת נִבְרָא הָעוֹלָם** — *Ba-ʿasarah maʾamarot nivra ha-ʿolam.*
>
> With ten utterances the world was created. And what does this teach, for surely
> it could have been created with one utterance? But this was so in order to punish
> the wicked who destroy the world that was created with ten utterances, and to give
> a good reward to the righteous who maintain the world that was created with ten
> utterances.

**Why "ten"?** Read Genesis 1 and count the times "**And God said**" issues a
command — there are **nine**. The rabbis add the opening word **"In the beginning"**
(*Bereshit*) as itself a creative utterance (so the Talmud, *Rosh Hashanah* 32a:
"*Bereshit* is also an utterance") — and the total is **ten**. The point is not
arithmetic for its own sake: the rabbis are reading Genesis 1 as a *deliberately
counted composition*. The structure is the message.

## 5:6 — Ten things made at twilight on the eve of the first Sabbath

> Ten things were created on the eve of the Sabbath at twilight, and these are they:
> [1] the mouth of the earth, [2] the mouth of the well, [3] the mouth of the
> donkey, [4] the rainbow, [5] the manna, [6] the staff [of Moses], [7] the shamir,
> [8] the letters, [9] the writing, [10] and the tablets.

Even the "loose ends" of the later story — miracles that seem to break the rules —
are gathered up and *counted into the creation week*, slipped in at its last
twilight. Nothing is left uncounted.

## The shape of the whole chapter — a poem of numbers

Chapter 5 keeps counting, in a descending cascade:

- **Ten** utterances of creation (5:1)
- **Ten** generations from Adam to Noah (5:2)
- **Ten** generations from Noah to Abraham (5:3)
- **Ten** trials of Abraham (5:4)
- **Ten** miracles in the Temple, and **ten** plagues in Egypt, and **ten** wonders at the sea (5:5)
- **Ten** things made at twilight on Sabbath eve (5:6)
- then **seven** marks of a wise person, and **seven** kinds of calamity (5:7–8)
- then sets of **four** — four temperaments, four kinds of students, four givers of charity (5:10–14)

The same two numbers that organize Genesis 1 — **ten** (the utterances) and
**seven** (the days) — organize the rabbis' own meditation on it. They did not
receive Genesis as a flat chronicle to be measured against a clock. They received
it as a *patterned, countable, poetic* text, and they answered it in kind.
