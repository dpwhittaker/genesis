## Part One — The Creation of Humankind (Old Babylonian Tablet I)

> *Akkadian transliteration: eBL (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0; credited above). English: a fresh working translation made here, line-numbered to match the Akkadian. In the transliteration, `|` marks a metrical foot and `||` the caesura; `[ ]` is text broken on the tablet, `( )` a word supplied for sense, `x` an unread sign, `{d}` the "divine" marker before a god's name. In the translation, `[ ]` flags a restoration, `(?)` an uncertain rendering, and `…` a gap.*

This is the opening tablet of the Old Babylonian epic — the version that begins with the famous line *"When the gods were man."* Before humans exist, the lower gods (the **Igigi**) do all the world's labor for the high gods (the **Anunnaki**). They go on strike. The solution the gods reach is to **create humankind to carry the work** — and they make the first humans out of clay mixed with the flesh and blood of a slaughtered god, so that something of the divine, a *ghost/spirit* (**eṭemmu**), lives on inside every person. Compare Genesis 2:7, where the human is formed from the dust of the ground and the breath of God.

### Enki's plan: make a human to bear the load (iii 44 – iv 6 // 174–217)

### The slaying of Wê-ila — clay, flesh, blood, and the surviving spirit (iv 1' – v 4 // 205–227)

This is the heart of the tablet. A god named **Wê-ila** — *"the god who had ṭēmu,"* that is, who had reason, intelligence, the spark of personhood — is the one slaughtered. His flesh and blood are kneaded into the clay, so that an *eṭemmu* — a ghost, a living spirit — remains forever inside humankind. The pun is deliberate: many read the human word *awīlu* ("man") as echoing **Wê-ila**, the god who died to make him.

### The first births (v 24 – vi, fragmentary // 247–280+)

> The womb-goddesses are gathered. Nintu pinches off **fourteen pieces of clay** — seven to the right, seven to the left — and sets a brick between them. She marks out the human form. Marriage is established between man and woman; the ten months of pregnancy are counted; and when the tenth month comes, the womb is opened and the first humans are born. From here the tablet turns to what comes *next* — humankind multiplies, the land grows loud, and the gods, disturbed, begin to send the plagues and famine that lead toward the flood.

## Part Two — The Famine and the Flood: a public-domain witness (George Smith, 1876)

> **⚠ Honesty note — please read first.** *No complete pre-1929 public-domain translation of the* Atrahasis *epic exists.* The epic as we know it today was **reconstructed in the 20th century** by joining tablets and fragments that earlier scholars treated as separate texts; the standard complete edition and translation is **W. G. Lambert & A. R. Millard, *Atra-ḫasīs: The Babylonian Story of the Flood* (Oxford, 1969), which is still in copyright.** Likewise every modern translation (Stephanie Dalley, Benjamin Foster, etc.) is copyrighted and is **deliberately not reproduced here.**
>
> What follows is the **best legitimately public-domain material**: George Smith's 1876 renderings of the very Babylonian fragments that the Atrahasis epic is built from. In Smith's day the hero's name was read **"Atarpi"** and **"Adra-khasis"** (= **Atra-ḫasīs**, "exceeding wise"), and the flood story was known only as part of the eleventh "Izdubar" tablet (the **Epic of Gilgamesh**). So these pieces are fragmentary, use archaic readings, and are not the polished modern reconstruction — but they are genuinely in the public domain.
>
> **Group members who want the full modern reconstruction should consult Lambert & Millard, *Atra-ḫasīs* (1969), or Stephanie Dalley, *Myths from Mesopotamia* (Oxford World's Classics).** Those are the texts to read for the complete creation-of-mankind-and-flood narrative; the material below is the historical, public-domain witness to the same tradition.

### Editor's orientation (Part Two)

Smith's book preserves two pieces that together correspond to the Atrahasis tradition:

1. **The Story of Atarpi** (Smith's "Atarpi" = **Atra-ḫasīs**) — a heavily-broken six-column tablet in which the gods, angry at "the sin of the world," resolve to destroy mankind, first by **famine and drought**, while a righteous man intercedes. This matches the *plague/famine* episodes that precede the flood in the Atrahasis epic.
2. **The Story of the Flood** (eleventh tablet of the Izdubar/Gilgamesh series) — the flood narrative proper, in which the survivor is named **Adra-khasis** (= Atra-ḫasīs), builds the ship, rides out the deluge, lands on Mount Nizir, sends out the birds, sacrifices, and is finally **translated to dwell "at the mouth of the rivers."**

Smith's own gap markers are preserved throughout: `....` / `......` mark broken signs within a line, a dashed rule (`— — — — —`) marks the boundaries between repeated or separated passages, and notes such as *"(Several lines lost.)"* mark larger breaks. Smith's bracketed `( )` are his own restorations; `[ ]` are his alternative readings.

### A · The Story of Atarpi — the famine (Atra-ḫasīs)

*From* The Chaldean Account of Genesis, *Chapter X, "Fragments of Miscellaneous Texts." Smith presents an outline of the badly-mutilated tablet, with the surviving lines translated in full.*

A number of stories of a similar character to those of Genesis, though not directly connected with the latter, have been included in this chapter, together with two fragments which probably relate, the one to the Tower of Babel, the other to the destruction of the cities of the Plain. The first and principal text is the story of Atarpi, or Atarpi nisu, “Atarpi the man.” This story is on a tablet in six columns, and there is only one copy of it. It is terribly mutilated, very little being preserved except Column III., but there are numerous repetitions throughout the text. The inscription has originally been a long one, probably extending to about 400 lines of writing, and the text differs from the generality of these inscriptions, being very obscure and difficult. In consequence of this and other reasons, only an outline of most of the story is given here.

We are first told of a quarrel between a mother named Zibanit and her daughter, and that the mother shuts the door of the house, and turns her daughter adrift, the words of the original being “the mother to the daughter opens not her door.” The doings of a man named Zamu have some connection with the affair, his “descending into the street on getting” something being mentioned immediately before the expulsion of the daughter; and at the close we are told of Atarpi, sometimes called Atarpi-nisu, or Atarpi the “man” who had his couch beside the river of the north, and was pious to the gods, but took no notice of these things. When the story next opens, we find the god Bel calling together an assembly of the gods his sons, and relating to them that he is angry at the sin of the world, stating also that he will bring down upon it disease, tempest, distress, madness, burning and sickness. This is followed by the statement that these things came to pass, and Atarpi then invoked his god Hea to remove these evils. For a whole year, it would seem, he interceded for the people, and at last Hea answered, and announced his resolve to destroy the people. After this the story reads:

> 3. .... shall not stretch until before he turns.
> 4. Their famine I observe,
> 5. their shame the woman takes not;
> 6. I will look to judge the people?

### B · The Story of the Flood (Adra-khasis)

*From* The Chaldean Account of Genesis, *Chapter XVI, "The Story of the Flood and Conclusion." This is Smith's translation of the eleventh tablet of the Izdubar (Gilgamesh) series. The survivor of the flood is named* Xisuthrus *(the Greek form from Berosus) and, in the cuneiform,* **Adra-khasis** *(= Atra-ḫasīs). Only the flood narrative itself is given below — Columns I–IV, ending where Adra-khasis is taken away to dwell with the gods. (The remainder of the tablet, Columns IV–VI, returns to the Gilgamesh frame-story — the cure and homeward journey of the hero "Izdubar" — and is not part of the flood/Atrahasis material.)*

The eleventh tablet of the Izdubar series is the one which first attracted attention, and is certainly the most important on account of its containing the story of the Flood. This tablet is the most perfect in the series, scarcely any line being entirely lost. A new fragment of it, belonging to another edition of the story, has been recently brought to the museum by Mr. Hormuzd Rassam.
