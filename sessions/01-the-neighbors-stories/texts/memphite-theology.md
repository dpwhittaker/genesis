# The Memphite Theology — Egyptian Creation on the Shabaka Stone

> **Translator:** James Henry Breasted.
> **Year:** 1912.
> **Source work:** *Development of Religion and Thought in Ancient Egypt: Lectures Delivered on the Morse Foundation at Union Theological Seminary* (New York: Charles Scribner's Sons, 1912), pp. 43–47.
> **Source URL:** [https://archive.org/details/developmentofrel00brea](https://archive.org/details/developmentofrel00brea) (full text: [developmentofrel00brea_djvu.txt](https://archive.org/stream/developmentofrel00brea/developmentofrel00brea_djvu.txt)).
> Breasted first published a facsimile of the stone and a preliminary "rapid sketch" of its contents in the *Zeitschrift für ägyptische Sprache*, vol. 39 (1901), pp. 39 ff. ("The Philosophy of a Memphite Priest").
> **Public domain.** Published in the United States before 1929.

The text below is the so-called **Memphite Theology of Creation**, preserved on a mutilated stela in the British Museum (Stela No. 797, formerly No. 135). In the eighth century B.C. the priestly scribes of King Shabaka had a worm-eaten papyrus, then already crumbling, copied onto this stone; the stone was later re-used as a nether millstone, which nearly abraded the inscription away. The cosmogony it preserves makes **Ptah** of Memphis the primeval creator who brings the world into being first as a thought conceived in his "heart" (mind) and then as a thing made real by the command of his "tongue" (word) — a doctrine Breasted compared to the "And God said" of Genesis and to the later Alexandrian "Logos." What follows reproduces Breasted's English rendering of the creation passages (British Museum Stela No. 797, ll. 48–61), together with the short connecting sentences in which he framed each block. Section headings are editorial.

---

## "He is the heart and tongue of the ennead"

One of the members of the Memphite ennead bears the name "Ptah the Great," and to this name is appended the remarkable explanation,

> "he is the heart and tongue of the ennead,"

meaning of course the Memphite ennead. This enigmatic "heart and tongue" are then identified with Atum, who, perhaps operating through other intermediate gods, accomplishes all things through the "heart and tongue." From Ptah then proceeded

> "the power of mind and tongue,"

which is the controlling power in

> "all gods, all men, all animals, and all reptiles, which live, thinking and commanding that which he wills."

## Creation by the thought of the heart and the command of the tongue

After further demonstrating that the members of Atum, especially his mouth which spake words of power, were made up of the ennead of Ptah, and thus of Ptah himself, our thinker passes on to explain his conception of the function of "heart (mind) and tongue":

> "When the eyes see, the ears hear, and the nose breathes, they transmit to the heart. It is he (the heart) who brings forth every issue, and it is the tongue which repeats the thought of the heart. He fashioned all gods, even Atum and his ennead. Every divine word came into existence by the thought of the heart and the commandment of the tongue. It was he who made the kas and [created] the qualities; who made all food, all offerings, by this word; who made that which is loved and that which is hated. It was he who gave life to the peaceful and death to the guilty."

## Creation of the world of material things

After this enumeration of things chiefly supermaterial, of which the mind and the tongue were the creator, our Memphite theologian passes to the world of material things:

> "It was he who made every work, every handicraft, which the hands make, the going of the feet, the movement of every limb, according to his command, through the thought of the heart that came forth from the tongue."

## The saying of Atum concerning Ptah-Tatenen

> "There came the saying that Atum, who created the gods, stated concerning Ptah-Tatenen: 'He is the fashioner of the gods, he, from whom all things went forth, even offerings, and food and divine offerings and every good thing! And Thoth perceived that his strength was greater than all gods. Then Ptah was satisfied, after he had made all things and every divine word.'"

## Ptah fashions the gods, the cities, and the nomes

> "He fashioned the gods, he made the cities, he settled the nomes. He installed the gods in their holy places, he made their offerings to flourish, he equipped their holy places. He made likenesses of their bodies to the satisfaction of their hearts. Then the gods entered into their bodies of every wood and every stone and every metal. Everything grew upon its trees whence they came forth. Then he assembled all the gods and their kas (saying to them): 'Come ye and take possession of "Neb-towe," the divine store-house of Ptah-Tatenen, the great seat, which delights the heart of the gods dwelling in the House of Ptah, the mistress of life … whence is furnished the "Life of the Two Lands."'"

*(British Museum, Stela No. 797, formerly No. 135, ll. 48–61.)*

---

## Breasted's closing reflection

> In this document we are far indeed from the simple folk-tales of the origin of the world, which make up the mythology of Egypt. Assuming the existence of Ptah in the beginning, the Memphite theologian sees all things as first existing in the thought of the god. This world first conceived in his "heart," then assumed objective reality by the utterance of his "tongue." The utterance of the thought in the form of a divine fiat brought forth the world. We are reminded of the words in Genesis, as the Creator spoke, "And God said." Is there not here the primeval germ of the later Alexandrian doctrine of the "Logos"?

## Translator's notes (Breasted's footnotes)

- On "thinking and commanding that which he wills": *"The verbal form of 'thinking' is questionable, but no other interpretation seems possible. Whether 'he' in 'he wills' refers to Ptah directly or to the 'power of mind and tongue' is not essential, as the latter proceeds from Ptah."*
- On the pronouns "he" (heart) and "it" (tongue): *"Heart and tongue have the same gender in Egyptian and the pronoun may equally well refer to either. I use 'he' for heart and 'it' for tongue, but, I repeat, the distinction is not certain here."*
- On "[created] the qualities": the bracketed word is Breasted's restoration of a damaged passage. The "qualities" render Egyptian *Hmswt*, which (as Brugsch showed) indicates the qualities of the Sun-god, here attributed in origin to Ptah: *"Might, radiance, prosperity, victory, wealth, plenty, augustness, readiness or equipment, making, intelligence, adornment, stability, obedience, nourishment (or taste)."*

> Note on the text: brackets `[ ]` mark words Breasted supplied where the stone is damaged; the ellipsis `…` in the final passage marks a lacuna (a worn or illegible gap) in the original inscription. Breasted's rendering quotes the substantive creation passages of the stela (ll. 48–61); the badly worn monument as a whole is not translated line-for-line.
